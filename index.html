British Wildlife Conservation

Who We Are

British Wildlife Conservation (BWC) is a British charity established in 2020. The BWC specialises in the conservation of some of the most endangered and important native british species. We are a team of 10 from all over the United Kingdom from the Isle of White to Galloway. We are passionate about nature and hopefully with your help we can raise awareness about the most endangered animals and their habitats.

Our mission is to raise awareness about the most endangered and important wildlife found in the UK from the honey bee to the Scottish wildcat. We rely on donations from like-minded people who also want to see habitats protected.

What We Do

We at the BWC get involved in many aspects of conservation from petitioning governments to advising local councils. We also release our popular free newsletter once a month containing information about Britain’s most endangered species, habitats at risk and the work we have done.Click here to sign up to our newsletter.

Species

At BWC we raise awareness for the most endangered and the important species to our ecosystem. These include:

The Scottish Wildcat Felis silvestris - whilst looking like a large household cat the Scottish Wildcat has thicker fur and a shorter blunt tail. Once native to the whole of the British Isles the Wildcat can now only be found in Scotland. There are now approximately 200 wildcats left. There have been recent efforts to release more cats into the Scottish forests in 2024.

The Eurasian Beaver Castor fiber - Is Britain’s largest rodent, whilst slow and cumbersome on land the beaver is an excellent swimmer and dam builder. Beavers were once common in Britain but were hunted to extinction for their fur. Recent efforts have led to the beaver being given protected status and reintroduced to certain areas of England and Scotland.

The Honey Bee Apis mellifera - The honey bee is a key pollinator in our environment and they are crucial to agriculture. Without the honey bee we would not be able to produce the variety and amount of goods we see on the shelves at supermarkets. Whilst you may see far more honey bees than beavers or wildcats, their numbers have dwindled since 1950 when figures were first published.
